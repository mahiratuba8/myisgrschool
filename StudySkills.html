Study Methods

Feynman technique

Provides a deeper understanding by explaining complex concepts in simple terms. This means you can teach someone the idea, even if you have little knowledge.

Active Recall

This technique encourages bringing up information from a while ago through your memory. It's like quizzing yourself; it strengthens your memory through the process of learning the subject. One should recall what they have learned for a small amount of time each day.

Past Papers

Looking over past test papers or formatives will help you become comfortable with the structure or types of questions you might encounter. You can understand where you went wrong. Make sure to make a note of mistakes and solutions, and try to understand the patterns of the questions to prepare for what is most likely to come up on the next test. If you don't have past papers, try finding someone to give you a practice test on the subjects and topics you're learning. This is where you shouldn’t be afraid to use ChatGPT; it can sometimes be useful in these situations.

Flashcards

They are a very popular tool people tend to use. They are certainly useful for memorising facts, definitions, dates, and formulas. The best combination can be active recall and flashcards to enhance your memory, so by the time your tests come up, you are already familiar with most of the things we usually end up looking over and memorising at the last minute.

Mind maps

They are a great tool for brainstorming and collecting what you know. They are best used when writing an essay, even if it’s an in-class essay where you have a limited amount of time. It is best to jot down ideas; it helps you to recall and not lose track when writing. Creating a mind map can also guide you on how to begin and end and formulate an answer while connecting your points. This is also great if you are a visual learner and want to help remember or understand concepts that involve lots of steps and connections.

Social learning

It sounds exactly like how it is—it can be studying with friends, a group of students, or even your siblings. But be in a group of people who will help each other stay on task and motivated. This kind of study technique is great when there are projects debates or oral tests where you need to communicate your information aloud. Not only that, but you can use the Feynman technique to help them and yourself understand concepts.

Study Tips & Resources

Linking Information

Avoid memorising all the information, but instead, link the sub-topics/concepts. It can be hard memorising a bunch of things, especially when they seem to be known separately in our heads, and it would be easier to forget a whole concept. Linking information from previous ones will help us understand the information better and help us remember it more easily. For example, if there is a topic on how acid rain affects rivers, the next topic can be the science behind acid rain, you can write a little text above the new topic that you gonna cover to remind and help remember and recall previous information as well as link the information, and give you a deeper understanding.

Key Points

When taking notes and jotting down information in your notebook, maintain a section or a page for the formulas and definitions for all the topics you learn and have taken notes on. This will help you identify the key points.

Documenting Doubts

Document or note down the question, incorrect answer or a topic explained by the teacher that you might be confused about. Once you clarify the doubts with someone like your teacher make sure to write their explanation down.
