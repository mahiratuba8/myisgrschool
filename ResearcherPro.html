Writing Assignments & Research Tips

How to Create A Research Topics

Firstly, you must choose a topic and ensure it aligns with the guidelines or expectations your teacher has provided for you.

Narrow it down to a specific subtopic that you can focus on and expand upon in your writing assignment.

Conduct an overview search to gain a good understanding of what you will be getting into, and check with your teacher to verify if the topic is appropriate.

    Determining Credibility of Sources

    Written by experts

    Look for sources that have been written by experts in their field. Experts usually tend to have good background knowledge and experience and learning in that topic you might be researching, and you can figure that out by going to the About page where they would usually have most of the information about the members of that blog or website.

    If there is no dedicated author then focus on if the information has been published by a well-respected organisation like NY Times or Al Jazeera.

    Citations

    Most credible source will cite their work, which shows transparency lets readers fact-check and allows them to gain more sources to discover and research. It also indicates that the author or writer has done research and not just written what comes to their mind.

    Up-to-date information

    Here, we have a special interview with our school librarian Elin Wästlund, who has kindly took the time to answer some questions on research, and source credibility, identifying bias, and what student should aviod and look ot for when reseraching. She has given several presentations over this topics, which is why her expertise in plagiarism, copyright, research would make her an excellent resource for us students to navigate the right and wrong when working on our assignments.

    1. What are some key aspects to look out for to identify whether the source is credible or not?

    Some key aspects to look out for is if the information presented in the source is demonstrable and repeatable or based on objective facts and evidence. For example claims should be backed up by evidence, the methodology should be told in detail and statements supported. It is also important that the information is presented accurately and that the source contains citations. It is a real warning sign if a source is based on guesses, opinions, unproven figures, inaccurate presentations or citations.

    Never stop at the surface level when checking the reliability of a source! Just because it looks good doesn't mean that it is reliable.

    2. How can you tell if the author or publisher of the source is trustworthy?

    They should have an academic and professional background within the subject area, and also be working within this, to be counted as an expert. Where you are working does also count. For example, if you are hired by a recognized university, you will be counted as more reliable. If your let your publication go through peer review, examinations and approvals by other experts within the subject field, you are also counted as more trustworthy.

    3. What are some ways to identify bias and differentiate between constructive/helpful bias and harmful/unfair bias?

    Every source contains a perspective, so before using a source, check how objective it seems. A way to discover bias is to check how opinion-based a source is, that is if any personal viewpoints shine through. It is a good sign if the source is based on objective research and contains empirical evidence.

    To discover bias it can be helpful to check what organization or institution is behind the source. If you read about it, it might say what objectives it has, if it is to engage, persuade, inform etc.. It is also good to check if any funding has been involved and more. If there is any own interests and funding behind it, it is a warning sign.

    4. Cross-checking information is helpful but, what are some points to look out for if multiple sources might not be reliable even if they are all common to a common agreement?

    It can be good to look out for if a bias has been intentionally hidden, if the true purpose of the source (political, religious, commercial, social or other) has been masked. This can have been done to make information appear more widespread and organic (picked up from grassroot level) in origin than it is, hiding the sponsor of the source or organization or withholding other important information to decide the bias of a source. This whole phenomenon is called astroturfing.

    Look up quotations to check if they have been cited and interpreted correctly, and have not been taken out of their context.

    5. How to recognise the difference between factual information and opinionated information in a source?

    Factual information is useful to back up, for example an argument. It is objective, that is not biased, and reflects a research finding or multiple perspectives. Opinionated information can be used to persuade, distort, or reflect somebody’s thinking. It lacks evidence to back it up though, because it is based on subjective information. It represents someone’s or an organization’s or institution’s perspective or interpretation.

    Note: Not all information can be either factual or opinionated. Some fit into a grey zone between these categories, for example when information is backed up with just some facts.

    6. How can you confirm if such information is true or not through primary and secondary sources?

    Both primary and secondary sources can be reliable and peer reviewed. Strong research uses both types of sources. To distinguish between primary and secondary sources, check whether the authors did the research themselves, or whether they analyzed research done by others.

    In primary sources, the person writing the piece did the research, witnessed the event, or created something new. Primary sources therefore require you to examine and interpret original materials directly. A helpful way to apply source evaluation can therefore be to examine the historical and cultural context, check how the purpose and audience have been considered, evaluate potential biases and limitations and draw independent conclusions.

    In secondary sources, somebody evaluate or analyze what others have done, witnessed or created. Secondary sources therefore require you to evaluate how other scholars have interpreted these materials and the methods they used to reach their conclusions. A helpful way to do source evaluation can be to analyze scholarly interpretations, assess the source’s methodological frameworks, evaluate theoretical contributions and compare multiple perspectives.

    7. How do you assess the credibility of information that comes from blogs forums, and popular social media apps?

    At first you need to apply a general source evaluation test, like The 5 w’s: Who has written what for whom, when and why? In addition to that, you also need to check more things, since social media is not counted as a reliable source.

      For example you need to consider:
  • How old is the account?
  • What does the author's network look like? Do some background research of fans and followers: Who is in this network and who is following them? Who leaves comments?
  • How reliable is the author? What social media channels and / or web pages is the author running? What kind of content shows up on these pages?
  • What does the context look like? Does the author usually post about the topic? If so, what do past or updated posts say? Are details filled in as they occur?
  • How reliable is the content? Can the information be verified via other sources?
  • How geographically relevant is the source? If it features “breaking news” content, is the author near the location of the event?

    • 8. How to avoid using unreliable sources for research?

    The best way to avoid unreliable sources is to use scientific literature, such as found in encyclopedias, databases, nonfiction books and more. It also helps a lot to have a thorough understanding of what bias is, and keep in mind how bias affects your sources - and them in their turn - the quality of your school work. You also need to consider not only the bias of other people, but also your own, in the planning phase, research phase, data collection, analysis and presentation of your school work.

    If you understand what bias means, it will be much easier to think critically and independently, for example when examining your sources.

    9. What are common mistakes a student makes when analysing and evaluating the reliability of a source or in research in general?

    When analysing students have a hard time to differentiate between / know how to properly conduct the different activities syntesising, analysing, summarizing and evaluating sources. For example, you usually synthesize information after you have summarized and analyzed multiple sources, when you combine information from each source to create a cohesive idea.

    When you analyze information you examine elements and structure of a source, so you can more easily interpretat or discuss it. When you evaluate information you draw conclusions, for example about its reliability.

    In research in general, it can be hard for students to select relevant sources, according to the purpose of their school work, decide the length of a cited excerpt and both do in-text citations and citations properly to avoid plagiarism. A common mistake is also to forget to mention illustrators and translators in the works cited list. As a librarian I think that it is very important that the students know how and why they should use in-text citations and citations.

    How To Avoid Plagiarism

    CITE YOU'RE SOURCES!

    When writing and stating something that has been said or taken from another website, don’t forget to state the name of the source or the authors last name. Then make sure to elaborate further on that statement you used from a different source and write about how it connects to what you were writing assignment is about. Kind of like the SEE structure evidence and explain are very differen, and you dont want your explaination to be from somewhere else. Quotation is similar to citing, it makes it clear what you were stating is not your word but rather from another source, but don't forget to state from where those exact words you have quoted are coming.

    Paraphrase & Writing in you're Own Words

    Paraphrasing is something we do a lot when researching, but it's important to understand that it just doesn’t mean switching up the words, but writing the information into your own words also it requires for ou to understand and explain it in a new way without changing the meaning.
